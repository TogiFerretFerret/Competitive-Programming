Times when this is not necessary are when defining simple 1D vectors, when defining containers of non-primitive types (pairs, tuples, etc.) or when the container is not going to be defined with an initializer. 
In this case, you may use the standard constructor syntax WITH the custom datatype (e.g. matrix<type>, tensor<type> and hypermatrix<type> for 2d,3d, and 4D vectors respectively)
In case you have MULTIPLE SUBTESTCASES (e.g. Codeforces "T" testcases) use the MULTITEST macro. In case where the problem is interactive, use the INTERACTIVE macro.
For input and output, there are the in() and out() functions. These are powerful overloaded functions that can directly input into vectors, pairs, and vectors of pairs, in addition to working similarly to how cin and cout work (Except they do not get overloaded into)
Use them UNLESS impossible (e.g. printing one element after another with ' ' in between, which out() does not support as it directly outputs with a newline at the end). 
Out can also directly output vectors.
For loops that are NOT while loops (all for loops that do not reference a vector, e.g. `for(int& x: vec)`), there are three macros, forn, f0rn, and fOrn.

```
#define fOrn(v,s,e) for(int v=(s);v<(e);++v)
```
